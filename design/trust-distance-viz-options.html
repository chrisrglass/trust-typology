Visualizing Trust Distance

Four ways the results page could show how a person’s trust changes with distance, across the three questions. Same illustrative respondent in all four; every element is interactive. React to the shape of each idea, not the polish.

Illustrative data (no fielded respondents) · Palette validated for color-vision safety · Site design tokens

Option A

Your Trust Horizon

Three lines, one per question, from what you know firsthand to what you only know from a distance. The shape of each line is the finding: where your trust holds, and where it lets go. Hover for values; click the chips to isolate a line.

Emphasizes: the decay curve itself — the purest expression of the framework. Open question: is a line chart too analytic for a general audience?

Option B

The Ladder

Five rungs, from your own experience at the bottom to “colleges in general” at the top. Each rung shows your verdict on the three questions: trust (✓), unsure (–), or doubt (✕). Tap a rung to read what it means.

Far — judged from what you read and hear

Near — judged from your own life

Emphasizes: the concrete institutions at each distance; maps directly onto a future survey item. Open question: verdict symbols flatten nuance — is trust/unsure/doubt too blunt?

Option C

Your Trust Map

You at the center. Each ring is one step farther away; each wedge is one of the three questions. Darker means more trust. One glance shows whether your trust lives close to home, travels all the way out, or never leaves the house. Hover any cell.

Emphasizes: the “map” language the site already uses; the most shareable single image. Open question: polar charts are read impressionistically — fine for a portrait, weak for exact comparison.

Option D

Unexpected Company

Your line against each type’s typical shape, one question at a time. The marked bands are where you and that type agree — including types you would never expect. This is the “who you agree with might surprise you” promise, drawn.

Emphasizes: the empathy goal — agreement across division, independence within your own type. Open question: needs the most explanation; likely strongest as a second view after A or C, not the first thing seen.
