# Quiz Battery Finalization Recommendations

**Based on:** Pew-group pilot (N=100, 47 items) + naturalistic pilot (N=50, quiz battery)  
**Target:** Final quiz battery ready for fielded data collection  
**Date:** 2026-06-18

---

## What the Two Pilots Show Together

The Pew-group pilot tested discriminant validity under ideal conditions — pre-assigned respondents from nine maximally differentiated political typology groups. The naturalistic pilot tested the same instrument against 50 genuinely diverse Americans with no group labels. The two results converge on the same six drops and confirm the same classification logic. Where they diverge is in the G2 distribution, which signals a wording problem.

The key finding across both pilots: **trust in higher education is not one thing.** It is multi-dimensional, structurally organized, and not reducible to a liberal-conservative or optimist-pessimist spectrum. The quiz battery must capture three independent axes simultaneously.

---

## Part I: Items to Drop

Six items are removed from the quiz battery, for two different reasons.

### Floor Items (zero or near-zero discriminant validity)

These items produce near-universal agreement across both pilots. They cannot classify respondents because there is no variation to classify.

| Item | Quiz %B | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **D2-B** | 100% | Viewpoint suppression: universal agreement that campus culture discourages certain perspectives. Zero signal in both pilots. |
| **D3-C** | 100% | Values-practice gap: universal agreement that colleges don't live their stated values. Zero signal in both pilots. |
| **D3-A** | 98% | Leadership self-interest: only 1 of 50 respondents (2%) believes administration acts in students' genuine interests. Near-floor. |

D2-B and D3-C represent genuine consensus positions — things the American public has decided across political and demographic lines. They belong in a descriptive survey of public opinion but not in a classification instrument. D3-A has approached the same floor.

**Implication for the full survey instrument:** These items may still be valuable as toplines for press releases ("98% of Americans believe college administrators prioritize institutional reputation over student welfare") but should appear in a separate descriptive module, not the clustering battery.

### Redundant Items (high pairwise concordance with a better item)

| Item | Concordance Partner | Concordance Rate | Drop Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| **D5-D** | D5-B | 96% | D5-D (HE reinforces class divisions) and D5-B (too risky for low-income families) are effectively the same item. D5-B has slightly better variance (76% B vs. 80% B). Drop D5-D, keep D5-B. |
| **D2-A** | D2-C | 88% | D2-A (academic standards declining) and D2-C (intellectual formation doesn't happen) move together 88% of the time. D2-C has better spread (68% B vs. 80% B). Drop D2-A, keep D2-C. |
| **FA-A-trustees** | FA-A-administrators | Near-floor | 76% chose "none at all" on trustees. The faculty/administrator gap is the meaningful signal; trustees add almost nothing. Drop trustees from the quiz; keep faculty and administrators as a two-item scale. |

## Part II: Item-by-Item Status, Final Battery

### Confirmed Core Items (keep unconditionally)

These items showed strong discriminant validity in both pilots and provide essential classification signal.

| Item | Quiz %B | What it captures |
|---|---|---|
| **D2-F** | 40%/6%/54% | Primary classification axis: purpose (civic / moral-spiritual / career). Near-equal split between civic and career. The moral-spiritual option is the sole reliable identifier of the Faith Formation class. |
| **G1** | 54% | Global vs. domestic mission. Near-50/50 in naturalistic sample — the highest-quality binary split in the battery. Separates the civic-purpose cluster (IT, CR, MA) from each other via interaction with R-A1 and R-B. |
| **R-A1** | 84% | Self-reform capacity. Skewed (84% B) but the discordant cell (R-A1=A: 16%) is the entire Institutional Trustee class. No one who believes HE cannot self-reform also believes it needs only reform (not replacement). This item has zero false positives for the trustee class. |
| **R-B** | 66% | Reform vs. replacement. The essential fault line between the constructive critics (Critical Reformers, Institutional Trustees) and those who have given up on existing institutions. 34% still want reform, including virtually all of the civic-purpose cluster. |
| **D6-A** | 54% | Local embeddedness. Near-50/50; the single best item in the D6 block. Separates local trust (IT, CR) from the alienated majority. |
| **D6-B** | 66% | Research benefits my community. Adds seven unique discordant cases (D6-A=A + D6-B=B: "local institution is OK but research benefits flow away"). This captures a specific elite-skepticism that D6-A alone misses. Retain alongside D6-A. |
| **D2-C** | 68% | Intellectual formation. The better of the two standards items; retains more variance. The Institutional Trustee class (D2-C=A: 100%) is completely distinguished from the Credential Pragmatist class (D2-C=B: 100%). |
| **D5-B** | 76% | Mobility skepticism. High-income respondents split on this (43% B vs. 85% for mid-income). Captures both economic optimism at the top and economic anxiety across the distribution. |

### Structural Items (keep, with notes)

These items add classification power beyond what the core items capture, though some show weaker discriminant validity when considered in isolation.

| Item | What it adds | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **G2** | Discriminates within G1=domestic (C vs D split) | See Part III: wording revision needed. G2-C (restrict) vs G2-D (conditional) is an essential fault line within the domestic-first cluster, but 72% chose D, suggesting the current D option is too easy to agree with. |
| **G3** | Separates pure internationalists from security-concerned globalists within G1=A | 65% of global-mission respondents chose G3-A (collaboration first); G3-D (federal mandate) reaches across both G1 groups. Low-priority revision. |
| **R-A2** | Splits the replacement cluster (R-B=B) into pure disruption (D) vs. pressure-catalyzed replacement (C) | R-A2-D (replacement disruption, 44%) differs meaningfully from R-A2-C (both together, 44%). The C vs. D split separates the Mission-Aligned Abolitionists from Credential Pragmatists even when both chose R-B=B. |
| **D4-A** | Identifies Faith Formation class | 90% B (skewed) but the 5 A-choosers are not randomly distributed — they are healthcare workers and educators who see religious accommodation as genuine. Still useful for the FF class boundary. Consider wording revision. |
| **D6-C** | Identifies prestige investment within the global-mission cluster | All 11 prestige-invested respondents (D6-C=B) chose G1=A; but not all G1=A chose D6-C=B. D6-C discriminates within the global-mission group, separating Institutional Trustees from Critical Reformers and Mission-Aligned Abolitionists who are global-mission but not prestige-attached. |
| **FA-A-faculty** | Institutional actor trust — faculty end | Low variance (95% chose "2" or "3") but the faculty/administrator gap is consistent and meaningful across classes. |
| **FA-A-administrators** | Institutional actor trust — admin end | More variance than faculty. Mean ranges from 3.0 (IT, CR) to 3.7 (FF). Retain alongside FA-A-faculty as a two-item scale. |

## Part III: Wording Revision Recommendations

### G2 — Rebalance the Options

**Problem:** 72% of naturalistic-sample respondents chose D ("conditional on demonstrating domestic students not losing access"). Option D is too easy: it is procedurally reasonable and its condition is almost impossible to disagree with. This collapses the meaningful distinction between conditionally supportive (LP/OOL analog) and actively skeptical (LOL, NAR analogs).

**Recommended revision:** Sharpen option D by making its conditionality more demanding, and strengthen option A to attract the genuine internationalists who are currently forced into D as the least-bad option.

*Current D:* "International enrollment can strengthen universities, but growth should be contingent on demonstrating that domestic low-income students are not losing access."

*Revised D:* "International enrollment is fine where it already exists, but universities should freeze or reduce international numbers until they can demonstrate that domestic low-income students are gaining, not losing, seats."

*Current A:* "International students make American universities more competitive and contribute to research and innovation that benefits the broader economy."

*Revised A:* "American universities are stronger because they recruit internationally — the best students, researchers, and collaborators come from everywhere, and restricting that would make us weaker."

**Goal:** Pull genuine internationalists back to A and push ambivalent conditionals toward C or the revised D.

### D4-A — Reconsider Framing

**Problem:** 90% B in the naturalistic sample, and those choosing A (faith welcomed) are not meaningfully clustered — they are healthcare workers and educators who may be reading the question as "should faith be welcomed" rather than "is faith welcomed" in practice.

**Consider:** Reformulate as a belief-in-practice question rather than a perception-of-current-reality question. Or add a modifier that distinguishes conservative religious faith from generic spirituality, which is what the pilot personas understood as "faith."

## Part IV: Final Recommended Quiz Battery

**15 clustering items + 6 demographic items + 4 EX covariates = 25 total items**

This is comparable to Pew's 24-item final quiz.

### Clustering Battery (15 items, in recommended quiz order)

The ordering below moves from most accessible (economic, personal) to most structural (reformability, global mission), ending with the institutional confidence matrix. This minimizes dropout and ensures the most discriminating items appear early.

| # | Item | Type | Why here |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **D2-F** | 3-option | Opens the quiz with the primary classification axis (purpose). Non-threatening entry. |
| 2 | **D5-B** | Binary | Economic stakes — personally relevant, high completion likelihood. |
| 3 | **D6-A** | Binary | Local embeddedness — concrete and experiential. Near-50/50. |
| 4 | **D6-B** | Binary | Research benefits — paired with D6-A naturally. |
| 5 | **D6-C** | Binary | Prestige trust — completes the community/institutional section. |
| 6 | **D2-C** | Binary | Intellectual formation — the "does college actually work?" question. |
| 7 | **D4-A** | Binary | Religious inclusion — transitions to cultural alignment section. |
| 8 | **G1** | Binary | Global vs. domestic mission — the international section opener. |
| 9 | **G2** | 4-option | International enrollment stance — the most cross-cutting item; revised wording. |
| 10 | **G3** | 4-option | Foreign government involvement — completes international section. |
| 11 | **R-A1** | Binary | Opens the reform section: can HE fix itself? |
| 12 | **R-B** | Binary | If not: reform or replace? The key structural divide. |
| 13 | **R-A2** | 4-option | How would change actually happen? Splits the replacement cluster. |
| 14 | **FA-A-faculty** | Scale 1-4 | Actor trust — faculty. Part of the confidence matrix. |
| 15 | **FA-A-administrators** | Scale 1-4 | Actor trust — administrators. Closes the battery with the institution-as-actor items. |

### Demographic Items (6 items, appear after clustering battery)

| Item | What it measures | Classification use |
|---|---|---|
| **D-employment** | Current/most recent employment sector | Strongest demographic discriminant |
| **D-community** | Community type (city/suburb/small city/rural) | D6 items anchor |
| **D-income** | Household income (4 brackets + prefer not to say) | D5 items anchor; class equity analysis |
| **D-religion** | Faith importance (central/important/one factor/minimal) | FF class identification; D4-A interpretation |
| **D-age** | Age group (18-29, 30-44, 45-59, 60+) | Generational trend analysis |

|---|---|
| **EX-C** | How closely follows HE news (1–4 scale) |
| **EX-E** | How much direct experience shaped views |
| **EX-F** | Types of institutions with direct experience (multi-select) |

## Part V: Provisional Class Structure

The two pilots together suggest a six-class structure. These names and profiles are provisional — they will be revised after LCA on fielded data defines the actual centroids.

### Provisional Classes

**Institutional Trustees** (pilot: 20%)  
Civic purpose + global mission + believe reform is possible + favor both-internal-and-external reform strategy. All have bachelor's degrees or above. Mostly secular, high income, education and healthcare workers. The highest trust of any class on mobility (40% say too risky vs. 76-100% for others). The clearest anchor class — low confidence in administrators (admin trust 3.0) but retain faith in the overall mission and trajectory. The Loyal Liberal analog from the Pew typology.

**Critical Reformers** (pilot: 10%)  
Civic purpose + global mission + cannot self-reform + want reform not replacement. All have bachelor's degrees. Primarily educators and nonprofit workers. Believe in the mission and want it delivered but think external accountability is essential to force change. Admin trust: 3.0 (same as IT, different rationale). Distinguish from IT by R-A1: they have given up on internal reform capacity while still opposing replacement. The most policy-engaged class.

**Mission-Aligned Abolitionists** (pilot: 14%)  
Civic purpose + want replacement. Unusual profile: educated (71% bachelors+), bimodal income (low and high), mostly educators and nonprofit workers. Believe the mission is right but these institutions cannot deliver it. Moderate on mobility skepticism but high on community embeddedness skepticism. Admin trust: 3.3. Distinguishable from Credential Pragmatists by purpose (civic vs. career), global mission orientation, and education level. The key interpretive insight: they are not critics of the civic mission but of the institutions claiming to pursue it.

**Credential Pragmatists** (pilot: 42%)  
Career purpose + domestic mission + want replacement via disruption. The largest class by a wide margin. Diverse demographics but concentrated in trades, private sector, and working income ($40-79K). Near-universal mobility skepticism (95%), high community skepticism (62%), and universal belief that intellectual formation doesn't happen (D2-C=B: 100%). Admin trust: 3.5. They do not oppose higher education as a credential provider — they oppose its self-presentation as something more than that. Their desired replacement is market-based (alternative credentials, bootcamps) rather than institutional reform.

**Outcomes-Focused** (pilot: 8%)  
Career purpose + global mission. Smaller, internally coherent: private sector workers, secular, middle-income, mostly bachelor's. Moderate skepticism across the board. This class may not survive LCA as a stable category; its members share career-credential purpose but divide on R-B (reform vs. replace). Watch in fielded data for merger with IT or CP.

**Faith Formation** (pilot: 6%)  
Moral-spiritual purpose + domestic mission + want replacement. Small but distinctive: faith-central in religion, low income, nonprofit or not-employed. Maximum skepticism on every dimension (100% on D5-B, D6-A, D6-B). Admin trust: 3.7 (highest of any class). Desire replacement via external pressure (government) not internal reform. The FFC/NAR analog — but their concern is not primarily cultural hostility (though they see that too) but the absence of moral-formational purpose from the dominant institutional model.

### Open Questions for LCA

1. **IT and CR merger?** Both are civic/global/educated/professional. Distinction rests entirely on R-A1. LCA may merge them if other items dominate.

2. **MA distinctiveness at scale?** MA members divide across income and mission; LCA may split them into two sub-classes or merge the high-income half with CR and the low-income half with CP.

3. **FF at national scale?** Only 3 respondents; too small to profile reliably. Expect 6-10% in a national sample based on Pew benchmarks.

4. **G2-C restrictionists as a standalone class?** All 9 domestic-first/G2-C respondents chose R-B=B (replace) and were faith-diverse. They may constitute a "Sovereignty Skeptic" class in the national data.

## Part VI: What Comes Next

### Fielded Data Collection

Target: **400–600 respondents**, nationally representative on age, race/ethnicity, education, and geography. The demographic items are now part of the survey, so demographic weighting can be applied post-collection.

Use the 15-item clustering battery as the primary instrument. The 6 demographic items and 4 EX covariates appear as a closing section.

### LCA on Fielded Data

Run LCA with 4-, 5-, and 6-class solutions. Evaluate against external validity criterion (EX-D: willingness to support public funding; EX-G: own education level). Select the solution that produces the most externally predictive class structure, consistent with Pew's fitness criterion.

### Nearest-Centroid Classifier

After LCA defines centroids:
1. Compute each class's response profile across the 15 clustering items
2. Score any new respondent's distance to each centroid
3. Assign to nearest centroid
4. Return class name and description

The quiz UI then shows respondents their class, a one-paragraph description, and optionally how close they were to adjacent classes.

### G2 Wording Revision

Before fielding, revise G2 option D (and A) per Part III. The current wording produces a 72% convergence on one option, which degrades classification power.
