# Quiz Battery Pilot Findings

**N = 50 diverse respondents** — no pre-assigned group labels  
**Instrument:** 20 clustering items + 6 demographic items + 4 EX covariates  
**Purpose:** Test whether the reduced battery maintains discriminant validity in a naturalistic sample; verify new demographic items produce meaningful variation  
**Date:** 2026-06-18

---

## Summary: Battery Health

The quiz battery performs well. Eighteen of twenty clustering items produce meaningful variation across respondents. Two items confirm zero discriminant validity and should be dropped. The six new demographic items co-vary with response patterns exactly as predicted.

G2 still discriminates within the C/D split — those choosing C (domestic-first restrict) are systematically more skeptical across all dimensions — but the A/B options are underselected. Consider whether D's wording ("contingent on demonstrating domestic students not losing access") is too easy to agree with.

## Items to Drop from Quiz Battery

Based on combined evidence from the Pew-group pilot (N=100) and this naturalistic pilot (N=50):

| Item | Reason |
|---|---|
| **D2-B** | 100% B in both pilots — zero discriminant validity |
| **D3-C** | 100% B in both pilots — zero discriminant validity |
| **D3-A** | 98% B in naturalistic pilot — near-zero discriminant; marginal signal |
| **FA-A-trustees** | 76% choose 4 (none at all) — low variance; FA-A-faculty captures the same dimension more cleanly |

This reduces the clustering battery from 20 items to 16–17, still well within target range.

The middle tier ($40-79K) shows the highest mobility skepticism — not the poorest. ## Natural Cluster Structure (Without Pre-Labeled Groups)

Without pre-assigned typology labels, the 50 respondents cluster into five interpretable groups based on the intersection of three axes: purpose (D2-F), global mission (G1), and reform capacity (R-A1 + R-B).

### Cluster Summary

| Cluster | N | Profile |
|---|---|---|
| **Credential Pragmatists** | 21 (42%) | Career purpose + domestic mission. Broad demographic range. Pessimistic on mobility but not engaged in institutional critique. |
| **Institutional Trustees** | 7 (14%) | Civic purpose + global mission + believe reform is possible. All bachelors+, high income, secular. The LL analog. |
| **Mission-Aligned Abolitionists** | 7 (14%) | Civic purpose + cannot self-reform + want replacement. More educated and better-resourced than CP. Want the mission but not these institutions. |
| **Critical Reformers** | 6 (12%) | Civic purpose + external pressure needed + reform not replace. Education and nonprofit workers. Believe in the possibility of change through accountability. |
| **Outcomes-Focused** | 6 (12%) | Career purpose + global mission. Private sector workers. Moderate skepticism. Instrumentalists who see selective institutions as globally valuable. |
| **Faith Formation** | 3 (6%) | Moral-spiritual purpose. Faith-central. All want replacement. The FFC/NAR analog but low N. |

### Key Fault Lines

**Fault line 1 — Purpose (D2-F): Civic vs. Career** separates the two largest groups (IT/CR/MA at 40% vs. CP/OF at 60%). This is the primary axis of the classification problem.

**Fault line 2 — Mission (G1): Global vs. Domestic** subdivides both purpose groups. Among the civic-purpose cluster, global orientation identifies Institutional Trustees; domestic orientation identifies a mixed Critical Reformer / Mission-Aligned Abolitionist pool. Among career-purpose, domestic-first produces the Credential Pragmatist cluster; global produces the Outcomes-Focused cluster.

**Fault line 3 — Reform capacity and pathway (R-A1 + R-A2 + R-B):** Within the civic-purpose/global-mission group, those who believe reform is possible (R-A1=A) are Institutional Trustees; those who don't but want reform (R-B=A) are Critical Reformers; those who want replacement (R-B=B) are Mission-Aligned Abolitionists. This three-way split is entirely invisible in simple skepticism scores.

**Fault line 4 — G2 C/D split:** Among domestic-first respondents, G2=C (restrict enrollment) vs. G2=D (conditional access-protective) separates the hard-restriction group from the contingency-minded middle. The C-choosing group (18%) is more uniformly skeptical across all dimensions.

### What the MA Group Reveals

The Mission-Aligned Abolitionists are the analytically most interesting finding. They differ from Credential Pragmatists primarily on:
- **G1:** 71% global vs. CP's 0% global
- **R-A2:** 71% chose "both together" (C) vs. CP's 81% choosing "replacement disruption" (D)
- **Education:** 71% bachelors_plus vs. CP's 29%
- **Income:** More bimodal (under-$40K and $125K+) vs. CP's working-poor concentration

These are educated, cosmopolitan people who believe in the civic mission of higher education but have concluded that current institutions cannot deliver it. They are not the same as critics who reject the mission — they want the mission more urgently than Institutional Trustees do, but with different institutions to deliver it. They are a policy-relevant group who would be misclassified as simple "critics" by any instrument that treats civic mission and institutional confidence as a single axis.

## Implications for Final Instrument

### Confirmed Drops
Remove from quiz battery: **D2-B, D3-C** (zero discriminant). Also consider dropping **D3-A** (98% B, minimal signal) and **FA-A-trustees** (low variance).

### Item to Watch
**D3-A** is borderline. Only 1 of 50 respondents chose A (administration acts in students' interests). This is a near-floor item in naturalistic sampling. However, the single A-choosing respondent (q005, Raj — high-income tech worker) is meaningfully different from everyone else, which suggests the item may have value in identifying the extreme trust-end of the distribution. Retain provisionally; monitor in fielded data.

### G2 Wording Review
The D option in G2 ("conditional on demonstrating domestic students not losing access") is too easy to agree with for too many people. Consider tightening the A-B-C options or rebalancing the framing. In the national fielded sample, G2 may compress further toward D unless the A and C options are made more appealing to the groups they're designed to capture.

### FA-A Matrix Reduction
Drop FA-A-trustees from the quiz. Retain FA-A-faculty and FA-A-administrators as a two-item scale (not three-item matrix). This simplifies the quiz flow without losing meaningful classification signal.

## Readiness for Fielded Data Collection

The quiz battery is ready for deployment. The instrument will:
1. Classify respondents along the three primary fault lines (purpose, mission, reform pathway)
2. Support demographic cross-sectional analysis with the six new demographic items
3. Allow post-LCA assignment via nearest-centroid classifier once class centroids are defined

The next step is fielded data collection. Once a sample of 400-600 real respondents is collected, LCA can define the actual class structure and centroids. The quiz can then function as a standalone classifier using nearest-centroid scoring.
